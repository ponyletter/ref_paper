1 Introduction
As healthcare transitions to digital platforms, EHRs must be managed efficiently, 
securely, and with interoperability. Hospitals must preserve sensitive patient informa-
tion and swiftly access and assess it for medical decision-making upon the adoption of 
digital technologies [1]. Security protects patient data, while efficiency accelerates infor-
mation sharing. Interoperability enables healthcare systems and providers to exchange 
and understand health data irrespective of the platform used. This integrative strategy 
enhances care coordination, reduces administrative costs, minimizes medical errors, 
and ensures service continuity [2]. Contemporary healthcare ecosystems necessitate 
robust, scalable, and interoperable digital health systems to address increasing demands 
and enhance safety, responsiveness, and efficiency [3].

Abstract
Electronic Health Records (EHR) is the main core of modern healthcare, but 
interoperability across different blockchain platforms is a key challenge. This work 
proposes a cross-chain middleware architecture, which facilitates secure and real-
time synchronization of EHR data between Hyperledger Fabric (private blockchain) 
and Ethereum Sepolia Testnet (public blockchain). The framework integrates AES-
256 encryption and Inter Planetary File System (IPFS) as decentralized storage to 
enhance patient privacy. To facilitate interoperability across the blockchains the 
research introduces a smart middleware layer. This layer autonomously monitors 
the blockchain events, processes encrypted CIDs, enforces real time cross chain 
consistency and smart contract-based access control. The experimental evaluation 
shows that proposed framework achieves low synchronization times (< 195 ms), 
low gas and latency costs, small encryption overhead (< 4–5 KB), robust file storage 
and retrieval through IPFS. Such positive evaluations with scalable and real-time 
deployment, sets the foundation of patient centric interoperable healthcare 
ecosystems.

assisted healthcare communication [5], and sustainable AI in agriculture and medicine

management.
Traditional centralized systems, although historically utilized in healthcare, face sig-
nificant challenges that hinder single point of failure, scalability, and efficiency [7]. These 
systems also pose a major security and privacy risks, as centralized systems accumulate 
extensive amounts of sensitive data in singular repositories, making them attractive tar-
gets for cybercriminals. These risks not only compile personal information but also raise 
concerns over data ownership, censorship, surveillance, and potential manipulation [8, 
9].
Blockchain technology provides a decentralized, secure, and transparent framework 
for tackling challenges in healthcare data management. Blockchain can eliminate data 
silos, enhance interoperability, strengthen privacy, and reduce the risk of data breaches. 
Blockchain technology, through smart contracts, can automate operations such as 
consent management and insurance claims, allowing healthcare providers to securely 
exchange patient data without reliance on a central authority [10]. The unalterable char-
acteristics of blockchain ensure that medical records remain secure from tampering, 
while people gain increased authority over their health information, fostering confidence 
and involvement [7]. Blockchain technology offers a feasible alternative for creating 
interoperable, patient-centric healthcare ecosystems that are more efficient, secure, and 
transparent.
Nonetheless, despite its potential, most existing blockchain-based healthcare solu-
tions are limited to specific blockchain platforms. This insular approach hinders the 
achievement of true interoperability among diverse healthcare companies, leading to 
fragmented patient information and inefficiencies in care coordination [11]. Without 
cross-chain communication, healthcare professionals face difficulties in acquiring com-
plete patient histories, leading to treatment delays and unnecessary testing. Further-
more, single-platform applications often fail to leverage the unique benefits of various 
blockchain technologies, such as scalability, privacy features, and consensus mecha-
nisms. To fully exploit the potential of blockchain in healthcare, there is an increas-
ing demand for interoperable solutions that can connect diverse blockchain platforms, 
enabling safe and real-time exchange of health data across multiple organizational and 
technological obstacles [12].
Single-chain architectures in healthcare systems face significant limitations, includ-
ing poor scalability, high costs, and inadequate cross-institutional interoperability [13]. 
As healthcare transactions and data volumes increase, individual blockchains often 
face challenges due to the elevated demand, leading to processing delays and network 
congestion. Increased operational and computational costs exacerbate these problems, 
especially when all data and smart contracts must be processed on a single platform 
[14]. Furthermore, incompatible single-chain systems obstruct the sharing of patient 
data between healthcare organizations, resulting in fragmented medical records and 
incomplete patient histories. These limitations impede single-chain systems from fully 
realizing the potential of blockchain’s secure, transparent, and coordinated data-sharing 
capabilities.

Interoperability between blockchains, particularly for cross-chain electronic health 
record sharing, is crucial for enabling seamless transmission of patient data among 
diverse healthcare providers and regions [15]. Presently, patients often receive treat-
ment from many institutions using various blockchain systems. Without interoper-
ability, patient records are fragmented, leading to impediments in continuity of care, 
medical inaccuracies, and delays in treatment [16]. Cross-chain blockchain solutions 
provide secure communication, verification, and synchronization of health data, pro-
viding authorized healthcare practitioners with timely and accurate information [17]. 
This enhances clinical decision-making, bolsters patient safety, and increases healthcare 
efficiency. Interoperable blockchain networks can eliminate data silos, creating a more 
interconnected and patient-centric healthcare ecosystem that fosters improved out-
comes and increased care coordination globally [18].
To meet the increasing demands of modern healthcare systems, there is a critical 
need for secure, scalable, and real-time cross-chain EHR sharing frameworks that pro-
tect patient privacy while enabling swift and efficient data access [1, 19]. A framework 
facilitating uninterrupted access to medical records, irrespective of the blockchain plat-
form, is essential, as patients often receive care from several physicians across various 
locations and institutions. Traditional single-chain or compartmentalized systems lack 
essential interoperability, resulting in fragmented data and delayed clinical choices. A 
robust cross-chain architecture must ensure the confidentiality of sensitive health data, 
handle significant data volumes, and operate in real-time for important medical scenar-
ios [20]. Furthermore, these frameworks must prioritize patient security and adhere to 
stringent regulations such as HIPAA and GDPR while employing advanced encryption 
techniques [21, 22]. These concepts are crucial for creating an interoperable, patient-
centered healthcare ecosystem that enables rapid and accurate data sharing, hence 
enhancing care coordination and overall health outcomes [22].
This research proposes a comprehensive Cross-Chain EHR Sharing Framework that 
integrates many advanced technologies to address the critical need for secure, scalable, 
and efficient healthcare data sharing. A sophisticated middleware synchronization layer 
orchestrates Hyperledger Fabric, Ethereum Sepolia Testnet, IPFS decentralized storage, 
and AES encryption.
Hyperledger Fabric, a permissioned blockchain, guarantees secure and confidential 
transactions within trustworthy healthcare institutions, safeguarding sensitive health 
information. The Ethereum Sepolia Testnet, a public blockchain platform, enables insti-
tutions to safely exchange electronic health records across blockchain systems. IPFS 
(Inter Planetary File System) ensures the secure distributed storage of extensive health-
care data files without reliance on centralized servers.
Sensitive patient data is encrypted using AES during transmission and storage. Finally, 
a smart middleware synchronization layer ensures blockchain platform compatibility, 
enabling healthcare providers to share data and synchronize records. This innovative 
technology overcomes cross-chain EHR sharing technical and operational difficulties 
and maintains patient data safe, accessible, and compliant with healthcare regulations.

2 Literature review
Multiple research projects have investigated the utilization of blockchain technology in 
the management of Electronic Health Records (EHR), emphasizing data quality, prov-
enance, and access control [1–3]. These initiatives generally utilize blockchain’s immu-
table ledger to preserve a verifiable history of medical records and implement safe access 
protocols for approved healthcare entities. These solutions seek to mitigate the dangers 
of tampering and illegal data usage inherent in traditional centralized systems by decen-
tralizing control.
Simultaneously, scientists have developed machine learning systems of fraud detec-
tion [23, 24] and biometric-based security [25] that enhance the blockchain by increas-
ing trust and identity control in online ecosystems.
A common approach is employing Ethereum-based technologies for the management 
of electronic health records. Ethereum’s support for smart contracts enables automated 
consent management and dynamic access control [26]. Nevertheless, Ethereum’s reli-
ance on Proof-of-Work (PoW) or even Proof-of-Stake (PoS) consensus mechanisms on 
public networks leads to increased gas fees and limited scalability, making it inappropri-
ate for real-time healthcare environments that necessitate low-latency access to patient 
data [27, 28].
Beyond the performance costs, Ethereum’s security model is based on economic 
incentives between untrusted validators using cryptographic PoS mechanisms to 
ensure openness. But it also has variable transaction fees and probabilistic finality. This 
decentralized public model is very different from permissioned blockchains such as 
Hyperledger Fabric that rely on certificate-based identity and modular consensus proto-
cols (such as Raft or Kafka). In Fabric, access to the security is controlled through access 
control lists and endorsement policies instead of anonymous validators, which allows for 
deterministic execution. It facilitates very high throughput in trusted consortium net-
works. However, lack of interoperability with public blockchains limits to extend trust 
beyond consortium members.

and real-time performance goals addresses gaps of previous systems.
Conversely, Hyperledger Fabric, a permissioned blockchain platform, has been utilized 
in numerous research and deployments to facilitate private healthcare networks [39].

Research on cross-chain technologies, particularly in token transfers and decentral-
ized finance, has proliferated swiftly [44, 45]. Surveys and interoperability frameworks 
such as Cosmos, Polkadot, and blockchain bridges focus digital asset transfer mecha-
nisms. Nevertheless, the exchange of sensitive data, particularly in healthcare, where 
privacy and real-time access are paramount, often overlooked [46].
Despite significant advancements, certain essential research shortcomings remain. 
Existing systems currently lack effective and real-time cross-chain synchronization 
mechanisms suitable for the latency-sensitive demands of EHR access [47]. Secondly, 
privacy-preserving encryption techniques are not consistently integrated into block-
chain-IPFS solutions, leaving off-chain data vulnerable to exposure [48]. Third, there 
is an absence of integrated frameworks that combine the benefits of both Hyperledger 
Fabric and Ethereum, while also employing efficient decentralized storage (e.g., IPFS) 
and cryptographic protections such as AES [49, 50]. These inadequacies underscore the 
imperative for a unified, secure, and interoperable cross-chain framework tailored for 
healthcare data exchange.
2.1 Our contribution
This work is novel in cross-chain EHR sharing framework, which both accomplishes 
HIPAA/GDPR compliance and public auditability by combining the permissioned Role 
Based Access (RBAC) model of Fabric with the transparency of the Ethereum platform, 
and coordinates, via an event-driven middleware layer, with AES-256 + IPFS.
So, there are basic differences in consensus and security i.e. Ethereum: global trans-
parency and verifiability but at the cost of latency and throughput; Fabric: controlled, 
efficient and secure private execution but with limitations of interoperability. These dif-
ferences are sufficient motivation for our hybrid middleware solution. In essence, pro-
posed framework is designed to overcome the limitations of single-platform systems by 
simultaneously offering privacy-preserving permissioned data processing and public 
verifiability.
This study tackles the notable shortcomings in existing literature by presenting a low-
latency, privacy-preserving cross-chain framework for the exchange of Electronic Health 
Records (EHR). The design enables the secure and efficient transfer of patient data 
across various blockchain platforms, including Hyperledger Fabric and the Ethereum 
Sepolia Testnet. The application of Advanced Encryption Standard (AES) encryption 
ensures the confidentiality and security of off-chain health data stored in the Inter Plan-
etary File System (IPFS) against unauthorized access. The framework employs bidirec-
tional synchronization mechanisms to facilitate seamless data transfer between the two 
blockchain networks, enabling real-time interoperability while preserving the advan-
tages of each platform—Fabric’s permissioned access and throughput, and Ethereum’s 
public verifiability and smart contract capabilities. This comprehensive technique not 
only mitigates the limitations of isolated single-chain systems but also enables scalable, 
secure, and privacy-compliant electronic health record interchange across institutional 
and technological boundaries.
Such an integration of Fabric, Ethereum, and IPFS overcomes the shortcomings of 
earlier single-chain systems. Fabric keeps authorized, low latency transactions appro-
priate to clinical operations, and Ethereum offers public verifiability and transparency. 
IPFS is distributed storage of massive EHR files, which does not overload a blockchain,

3 Proposed methodology
The proposed research implements a cross-chain structure which enables encrypted 
EHRs to share and synchronize between multiple blockchain networks effortlessly. 
The system leverages both Hyperledger Fabric and Ethereum Sepolia Testnet networks 
which communicate through an advanced integration middleware combined with 
decentralized file storage system i.e. Inter Planetary File System (IPFS). The design pre-
serves privacy and availability combined with data consistency by using permissioned 
and public blockchains.
The EHR encryption process takes place through the user or doctor before an EHR is 
uploaded to IPFS. IPFS creates and returns a Content Identifier (CID) upon completing 
an encrypted file upload successfully. This CID enables secure reference to the stored file 
data. The CID functions as the central component for blockchain management although 
it never exposes medical information directly.
The system design features a smart middleware layer which tracks events broadcasts 
from blockchain-based smart contracts present on both Ethereum and Fabric networks. 
When the middleware detects events such as StoreEHR or FetchEHR, it initiates equiv-
alent transactions on both chains for synchronized operation. EHR metadata changes 
affecting one blockchain create near-real-time duplicates on the other blockchain 
because of the implemented cross-chain interoperability.

4 Components of the proposed system
4.1 Doctor/user
Doctors or users begin the process by making EHRs encryption at the local level shortly 
before storage. The encryption method protects patient-sensitive information before 
any external network receives it. The system makes blockchain complexities disappear 
through its feature of providing a straightforward user interface.
Before storing any EHR, it is encrypted with AES-256 encryption. Even if CID is 
exposed, the underlying EHR data will remain unreadable without the decryption key. 
Encryption provides a crucial additional layer of data privacy beyond what blockchain 
immutability offers.
4.2 IPFS network
The IPFS network stores encrypted EHR files with distributed and secure features 
including feature of tamper-resistance. The IPFS network uses CIDs as cryptographic 
hashes to refer to stored files. The CID serves as the only blockchain-stored data point 
because it protects private data by keeping it separate from blockchain storage.
The permanence of medical files in IPFS is guaranteed in the long term by pinning 
services (pinata gateway in our case) that avoid collection of garbage. In institutional 
deployments of production, it can be integrated with institutional clusters or incen-
tive-based systems including Filecoin to ensure that encrypted EHRs are durable and 
available.
4.3 Content identifier (CID)
The CID is obtained through IPFS which is used to represent the encrypted EHR. This 
pointer maintains a permanent status as an effective reference that enables quick data 
access without revealing the actual data contents. The lightweight nature of CIDs makes 
them suitable for blockchain transactions which in turn reduces the storage expenses on 
a blockchain.
4.4 Hyperledger fabric chaincode
The Fabric network deploys a specifically developed chaincode that handles two core 
operations.
  •
StoreEHR: The StoreEHR chaincode records the Fabric ledger with the CID and 
simultaneously generates an external notification event.
  •
FetchEHR: The FetchEHR chaincode retrieves EHR CIDs from Fabric ledger. This 
retrieval is based on access permission. The chaincode also triggers specific events 
upon permission approval.
Only authorized healthcare organizations and users can have access to EHR as Fabric 
network is permissioned blockchain.
4.5 Fabric ledger
Fabric ledger maintains permanent storage of EHR-related metadata that includes both 
CID and access control data. The private healthcare consortium network uses this sys-
tem to keep all transaction data secure while enabling version control. Hyperledger Fab-
ric network adopted Raft consensus to order. It is a protocol that is crash fault tolerant

and delivers deterministic finality. Validation is also enforced by the endorsement poli-
cies, and collectively it helps to provide Byzantine-resilient consensus suitable to per-

4.5.1 Ethereum smart contract (Sepolia Testnet)
A smart contract based on the Ethereum Sepolia Testnet functions identically to the 
Fabric Chaincode. It supports:
  •
StoreEHR: The system accepts and stores the CID with basic metadata through 
StoreEHR smart contract.
  •
FetchEHR: The FetchEHR smart contract acts as the interface for user access 
requests and emission of post-validation events. Using this smart contract EHR is 
fetched.
The system strengthens transparency between multiple stakeholders through its public 
blockchain deployment which provides decentralized trust features.
4.6 Ethereum ledger
Ethereum ledger ensures availability and immutability of data by storing CID mappings 
publicly. Even though the CIDs are public on the ledger, patient data confidentiality is 
ensured as direct exposure of sensitive information is prevented. Ethernet Sepolia Test-
net was employed in our implementation and functions under Proof-of-Stake (PoS) con-
sensus. This allows quicker block finality as well as reduced energy consumption than 
Proof-of-Work and maintains public verifiability.
4.7 Middleware layer
This layer performs orchestration of real time with bidirectional EHR synchronization 
between Fabric and Ethereum. It is also responsible for four core tasks:
  •
Monitors chaincode events such as StoreEHR and FetchEHR on Fabric.
  •
It monitors smart contract events on Ethereum for similar operations.
  •
On detecting an event on one blockchain, it triggers the matching transaction on 
the other blockchain so that the CID updates and retrievals stay in sync on both 
networks.
  •
It handles retry logic to do idempotent operations in case of transient failures or 
duplicate event emissions.
Extensibility is supported by the middleware layer with additional blockchains or health-
care systems able to be added.
In contrast to oracle-based or third-party bridging systems, cross-chain synchroniza-
tion of the proposed framework is accomplished by a custom of off-chain middleware. 
The middleware is subscribed to events on both chaincode of Fabric through gRPC and 
Ethereum smart contract events through Web3.js/ethers.js. Then middleware constructs 
and submits equivalent transactions on the other chain. The design does not depend

4.12 Synchronization workflow
In facilitating the secure, decentralized, and cross chain EHR exchange, the proposed 
system uses a orchestrated mechanism used to synchronize the Hyperledger Fabric, 
Ethereum (Sepolia Testnet), and the IPFS network. As far as the structure of this syn-
chronization workflow is concerned, its primary objective is that the operations of EHR 
storing, accessing and retrieving are actually placed on both blockchain networks con-
sistently but meanwhile ensuring the integrity, confidentiality, and interoperability of the 
health care data.
First a user-side function undertaken, that of AES to encrypt the EHR file containing 
sensitive medical information. Once encrypted, the file is uploaded to the IPFS network 
where a unique Content Identifier (CID) for it is created. Once this CID is sent to the

Cross Chain Middleware, it will act as a secure reference pointer and is recorded on Fab-
ric and Ethereum blockchains synchronously along with a series of coordinated opera-
tions by the Cross Chain Middleware.
Similarly, access permissions for the EHR are also managed and synchronized on both 
blockchain networks. It also empowers users to grant access dynamically, making autho-
rized entities in other networks to retrieve the encrypted EHRs from IPFS without any 
trouble. In terms of the decryption and final retrieval process, it is entirely user-centric 
and strictly data private.
In the Fig. 2, the detailed briefing of synchronization workflow across all the compo-
nents, EHR encryption and storage, permission granting and secure retrieval.
Step 1: When the user, either a doctor or a patient, wants to encrypt an EHR file, it 
locally encrypts the file with the AES encryption mechanism to ensure that the data will 
be confidential. Through a secure interface, the user uploads the encrypted EHR file to 
the Cross Chain Middleware. First, this encrypted file is uploaded by the middleware 
to the IPFS decentralized storage and obtains a CID that points to the file in the IPFS 
network.
Step 2: When the CID acquired from IPFS is ready, the middleware transmits a trans-
action on the blockchain. It triggers StoreEHR chaincode on Hyperledger Fabric and 
passes the CID as a parameter. The Fabric ledger securely makes the CID a part of some 
metadata, such as timestamps or the owner identity. Immediately after successfully stor-
age, the Fabric Chaincode emits a StoreEHR event indicating that a new encrypted EHR 
record has been committed to the ledger.

Step 3: The Cross-Chain Middleware listens for this StoreEHR event in real-time. The 
event is captured by the middleware which invokes the storeCID function on the Ethe-

sistency of the EHR pointer is achieved by storing the same CID on Ethereum.
Step 4: The user initiates requests in the middleware when another authorized indi-
vidual or healthcare provider wishes to access EHR. The GrantAccess function of the 
Hyperledger Fabric Chaincode is invoked with the middleware, causing an update of the 
access control records to allow permissions to be awarded solely to certain user identi-

advertising to external systems the permission change that has occurred. The same oper-

Step 5: The middleware detects the GrantAccess event and immediately synchronizes 
this permission information by calling the grantAccess function to the Ethereum Smart 
Contract. This guarantees in a fault tolerant, tamper proof way, that Fabric and Ethe-

the AES decryption key to locally decrypt the data, recovering the original EHR in order 
to consult, diagnose, or treat using it.
Throughout the entire workflow cross-Chain Middleware ensures that Hyperledger 
Fabric, Ethereum, and IPFS are synchronized, event handled, permission replicated, and 
data retrieval. This synchronization maintains a secure, transparent, and interoperable 
decentralized EHR management.

requirements like HIPAA and GDPR. Patient data is never stored on-chain in plaintext.

6.9 Potential bottlenecks and mitigation strategies
The above-mentioned observations, indicates promising results of the proposed frame-
work. But still there might be few bottlenecks while deploying it in real time. Following 
bottlenecks have been considered for this research.
  •
Infura Dependency (Ethereum Access): Infura endpoints have been used to 
communicate with the Sepolia testnet. Delay may occur because of infura rate limits 
or outages in case of large-scale usage. It is possible to reduce this through running 
special Ethereum nodes, or even through redundant provider services (e.g., Alchemy, 
Quick Node) to achieve availability.
  •
Fabric Channel Scaling Constraints: The performance of Hyperledger Fabric relies 
upon endorsement and ordering services. Consent overhead can increase along 
with the number of organizations or channels. This can be addressed by refining 
endorsement policy, using raft/Kafka endorsing service clusters and sharding patient 
data through several channels.
  •
IPFS Availability and Persistence: Despite IPFS having distributed storage, content 
availability is pegged on nodes that pin the files. When pinned nodes fail, the latency

7 Conclusion
This work presented in this paper is an integrated solution for cross-chain EHR interop-
erability that combines the strengths of private and public blockchains, off-chain IPFS 
storage, and lightweight AES encryption to ensure secure, real-time, and decentral-
ized health data management. By bridging Hyperledger Fabric and Ethereum through 
a smart middleware synchronization layer, the framework addresses critical gaps in tra-
ditional healthcare record management systems i.e. lack of interoperability, limited trust 
models, and privacy risks. The middleware’s capability to capture, process, and synchro-
nize events between blockchains ensures consistency without relying on centralized 
authorities.
Experimental results validate that the system maintains fast transaction invoke/query 
times, predictable gas costs, low middleware synchronization delay, and efficient encryp-
tion overhead, making it highly suitable for time-sensitive healthcare applications. The 
system thus offers a practical, scalable, and secure approach for cross-organizational 
health data sharing without relying on centralized intermediaries.
The proposed solution successfully demonstrates that blockchain interoperability, 
when combined with privacy-preserving off-chain storage and smart contract-based 
access control, can significantly enhance the efficiency, reliability, and scalability of EHR 
exchange networks. This lays a strong foundation for future decentralized healthcare 
architectures.
The proposed solution aligns with healthcare latency thresholds i.e. less than 500 ms 
and HIPAA / GDPR principles for confidentiality, integrity, and access control. Such 
alignment demonstrates strong potential for deployment in real-world EHR systems.
The framework bridges several important healthcare interoperability gaps through 
Fabric to ensure real-time, privacy-preserving, and verifiable interchange of EHR across 
heterogeneous networks; Ethereum to build transparency; and IPFS to provide decen-
tralized storage.
